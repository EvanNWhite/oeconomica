# Writing Up Results: From Analysis to Policy Memo

**Level:** Beginner
**Prerequisites:** None (conceptual, no code) — best used after
`02-data-analysis`

## Why this matters

The coding is in service of a memo, not the other way around. This page is
about the structure that turns "here's what I found in the data" into
something a policy audience can act on.

## Three types of policy answers

Know which one your project is aiming for:

1. **Descriptive** — What is happening? (states the facts/trend)
2. **Evaluative** — What is working? (assesses current policy)
3. **Prescriptive** — What should be done next? (makes a recommendation)

Most first-quarter research projects land closer to descriptive/evaluative;
a full prescriptive memo needs a stronger feasibility case (see below).

## Executive summary structure

**WHO and WHAT**
- Who is this for, and what will they use it for?
- State the problem concisely — either as a current-policy issue or a
  problematic situation

**WHY**
- Why does this matter now — what's the reason for looking at this?
- Signal the key options or approaches (sometimes just "status quo," or
  status quo vs. alternatives)
- Briefly mention pros/cons of the key options
- Briefly mention the method used (what data, what approach)

**HOW / WHEN / WHERE**
- Recommend a course of action, or state findings that could motivate
  future recommendations
- Give brief supporting reasons for that recommendation or finding

## Feasibility: does this actually matter, or is it purely theoretical?

Feasibility is what separates an idealized solution from something that
could actually be adopted. A common framework is **PEST**:

- **Political** — relevant regulations, taxation issues, government
  policy, political interests at stake
- **Economic** — inflation, business cycles, government spending, overall
  cost, consumer confidence
- **Social** — demographics, public attitudes, income distribution
- **Technological** — what technology is needed to implement the option,
  including energy use and availability

A **feasibility chart** (rows = policy options, columns = feasibility
criteria like political/administrative feasibility, equity, environmental
impact, cost) is a simple way to compare options side by side with
+/−/+/- ratings. A **stakeholder chart** does the same thing but with
columns for each interest group affected (e.g., public, industry, labor,
environment) instead of abstract criteria.

**SWOT analysis** is a related tool for assessing a single strategy or
institution: a 2×2 of Strengths / Weaknesses (internal) and Opportunities /
Threats (external).

## Practical tip

Don't try to fill in every cell of a feasibility or SWOT chart with deep
research — the point is to force the group to explicitly discuss
trade-offs they might otherwise skip. A quick "+/−/+/-" pass, discussed as
a group, is more useful early on than an exhaustive one nobody finishes.
